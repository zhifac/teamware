1. What GATE doesn't do

GATE helps...

  • ...scientists with:
    • repeatability
    • quantitative evaluation
    • collaboration
    • apparatus longevity
  • ...students and teachers with:
    • working examples
    • bootstrapping projects
    • evaluation baselines
  • ...application developers with:
    • comprehensive standalone class library
    • visual debugging tools

But:

  • applications fall into patterns
  • domain-specific software architectures promote application-level reuse
  • language tech deployment requires a process as well as tools

2. GLEAM: from Annotation Tools to Annotation Factories

We have lots and lots of tools and algorithms for annotation; what we need is

  1. methodological instead of purely technological
  2. multi-role instead of single role
  3. inclusive of previously disjoint adaptation interventions
  4. assistive instead of autonomous
  5. diachronic, life-cycle orientated, not waterfall or "big bang" orientated
  6. service-orientated, not monolithic

GLEAM, the GATE Layer for Extraction, Annotation and Mining, is a software suite and a methodology for the implementation and support of annotation Factory services.

2.1. Annotation: the Missing Manual

Processes vs. tools:

  • much literature describing algorithms, evaluation protocols and performance statistics for annotation
  • no clear statement of how to go about specifying and implementing annotation for new tasks

The GLEAM methodology covers:

  • how to decide if annotation is applicable to your problem
  • how to define the problem with reference to a corpus of examples
  • how to identify similarities with other problems and thence to estimate likely performance levels
  • how to design a balance of adaptation interventions for system setup and maintenance
  • how to measure success

In other words:

  • not "get this great new bit of software and it will transform your life"
  • but "this is how to implement robust and maintainable services"

2.2. A Division of Labour (Multi-role Methodology)

GATE has class libraries for programmers and an IDE for skilled language engineers, but there are other cases:

  1. human annotators:
    • training data volumes are typically large: the labour involved has to be cheap to be economic
    • annotation of training data for learning algorithms must require little skill
    • example techniques:
      • bootstrapping the annotation process with mixed-initiative learning
      • voting mechanism for multiple simultaneous annotators (QA)
  2. information curators:
    • larger skill set than annotators but training requirements small enough to be in-house in small/medium sized organisations
    • librarians, data curation or sys admin staff after training course of a week or two
    • use a richer toolset including things like ANNIC-based JAPE rule authoring
    • managing and convening human annotators, liaising with engineers

2.3. Unifying Adaptation Interventions
  • problem: annotation systems are dynamic objects because the information need and the data don't remain static
  • therefore we need mechanisms for adaptation
  • three current research streams:
    • assisted authoring of finite state transduction rules, including: generation of rules from annotation pattern searches across corpora graphical debugging of transduction rule execution
    • supervised mixed-initiative learning of extraction models
    • unsupervised clustering of terms for purposes such as populating domain-specfic gazetteers
  • GLEAM: a coordinated combination of these techniques

2.4. Assistive vs. Autonomous
  • A modern factory engaged in the production of high-tech goods combines a large degree of automation with skilled labour of various types and quantities.
  • Robots often play a significant role, although they are never altogether unaccompanied:
    • at the very least service engineers must attend their operation
    • in most cases there will also be staff who take care of reconfiguring robotic equipment for new product lines or refinements to the existing processes and products
  • GLEAM mixes automatic and assitive modes

2.5. Iterative Processes vs. Waterfall or "Big Bang"

How to construct a failing software project:

  • work for the government (optional)
  • define your plan as:
    • first we (highly-paid developers) decide what to build (requirements)
    • then we (middle-level developers) decide how to build it (design)
    • then we (lower-level developers) implement it (coding)
    • then we (support staff) deploy it (disaster)

A better model:

  1. get a rough idea of what the client needs
  2. decide on an element of 1. that we can do quickly
  3. implement a set of tests that will succeed if we build 2.
  4. build 2. until the tests work (adding more tests in the process and using a continuous integration tool)
  5. give it to the user; go back to 1.

The same with annotation: we need iterative methods that account for changing needs and gradual refinement of requirements.

2.6. Service-Orientated, not Monolithic
  • software has evolved: modules, objects, components, now services
  • increases decoupling and flexibility
    • GATE's component architecture led to long life
    • now we have ubiquitous networks and geographically distributed workforces
    • GLEAM's service-oriented architecture exploits distribution
  • Gartner reports that
    • "By 2008, SOA will be a prevailing software engineering practice, ending the 40-year domination of monolithic software architecture"
    • "Through 2008, SOA and web services will be implemented together in more than 75 percent of new SOA or web services projects."

3.3. Apps (3) The Return of the Librarian?

Some decades ago, if the chief engineer of a Sheffield steelworks needed to know the value of some constant in the equations describing the mixture of ingredients for a specialist job, he'd phone the city library. Even now, if you work for a large corporation you may well be able to call on the services of a skilled information scientist whose job is to know more about navigating the published universe than any normal mortal could ever learn.

These skills are less and less available. The winding down of the welfare state public service in favour of private profit has closed a thousand libraries, and the information firehose has cheapened information to the degree that the little query box on Google's search page has become the only investigative tool for many.

Team annotation tools can be a means to make librarian services affordable for small teams and organisations.
